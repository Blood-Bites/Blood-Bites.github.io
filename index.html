Thalassemia

A hereditary hemoglobin disorder

Description

    Our blood is composed of multiple elements, among these are our red blood cells. (Eleftheriou, 2013) Our red blood cells are responsible for transporting nutrients, the food of the cells, and oxygen, the fuel of the cells, for our everyday bodily functions. Red blood cells can carry oxygen through one of its components called hemoglobin. Hemoglobin has an affinity, or sticky factor, to oxygen, making it possible to be a carrier of oxygen. Moreover, hemoglobin can be observed to have a chain-like structue, specifically, it is made up of four chains, two chains of α (pronounced as alpha chains) and two chains β (pronounced as beta chains). Each chain has its own heme, which contains iron used to carry the oxygen. (Ashar, Miller, & Sisson, 2016)

    Thalassemia is an inherited blood disase, meaning that this is a blood disease passed on through the genes, or basically, by birth. (Eleftheriou, 2013) It cannot be transmitted through blood, air, or water, or can be caused by poor nutrition. In simple sense, thalassemia is the disease that results from the failure to synthesize or create this hemoglobin molecule in red blood cells. (Eleftheriou, 2013)









Causes

    The ultimate cause of thalassemia is hereditary. As stated in the description of thalassemia, it cannot be caused by poor nutrition, or medical conditions. It cannot also be transmitted from someone else who has thalassemia, be it through blood, air, or water. Thalassemia is caused by defective genes resulting to a failure to synthesize or create hemoglobin out of iron.

    The α chains and the β cahins, depending on the specific form of thalassemia may cause the certain cahins to be missing or produced less. In β-thalassemia major, either no β-chains are produced, or a very small amount, leaving only α chains. This imbalance severely affects the process of making red blood cells, found in our blood.
